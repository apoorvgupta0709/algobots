### 1.3 Regulatory and Environmental Context

The regulatory environment in which Cummins India operates is characterised by progressively stringent emissions standards and an evolving policy framework around air quality, decarbonisation and energy security.[29][37] India’s transition from Bharat Stage III to BS IV and subsequently to BS VI norms for vehicular and off-highway engines has significantly reduced permissible levels of nitrogen oxides (NOx) and particulate matter (PM), with empirical studies showing that the shift from BS III to BS IV alone resulted in NOx and PM emission reductions of around 30 percent and 80 percent respectively.[29] Cummins India has been at the forefront of this transition, leveraging the global Cummins technology base to supply BS VI-compliant engines and aftertreatment systems and positioning itself as a provider of cleaner internal-combustion solutions that align with India’s environmental objectives.[37]

A 2019 Cummins India communication emphasised the company’s role in “powering a cleaner and more prosperous India” through BS VI solutions, highlighting the deployment of advanced aftertreatment, electronic controls and improved combustion strategies to meet stringent emission ceilings while maintaining reliability and fuel efficiency.[37] This regulatory and technological trajectory has two implications for investors. First, it raises the technical and capital barriers to entry in Cummins India’s core markets, arguably strengthening the competitive moat of established players with global R&D backing and compliance experience.[29][37] Second, it embeds transition risk into the business model: while BS VI technologies enable Cummins to continue selling internal-combustion engines under tighter norms, further policy shifts toward zero-tailpipe-emission solutions—such as battery-electric or fuel-cell powertrains—could challenge the long-term volume potential of diesel-centric businesses, forcing a more rapid adoption of alternative power platforms.[30][43]

Cummins Inc.’s global sustainability and ESG disclosures reinforce this dual dynamic.[23] The group reports extensive environmental initiatives across facilities and operations, including greenhouse-gas reduction projects that together are projected to save tens of thousands of metric tons of carbon emissions annually, alongside investments in hydrogen, fuel cells, hybrid systems and other low- or zero-carbon technologies.[23][43] For Cummins India, these global commitments translate into both an opportunity to be a regional manufacturing and engineering partner for future technologies and a pressure to manage the gradual decline, over the very long term, of purely diesel-based revenue streams.[23][37][43] In the near to medium term, however, the interplay of stricter local emissions norms, continued reliance on diesel for heavy-duty and backup applications, and the gradual, targeted rollout of alternative-fuel solutions creates a complex but potentially favourable environment for technologically sophisticated incumbents.
